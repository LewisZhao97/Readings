Abstract-In this paper, an image guided depth super-resolution approach is presented for generating higher-quality and higher-resolution depth maps from lower-resolution depth points, such that it can be used in depth based image re-projection (a.k.a: Spacearp) and other applications in XR. In this approach, a higher-resolution depth map image is reconstructed using spatial, intensity, and depth information of neighborhood pixels. Three kinds of weights are computed with neighborhood information. The first one uses 3D pose information, the second one uses color image intensity information, and the third one uses color image spatial information. We compute the weighted sum of the depths in neighborhood and accumulate the weights. The depth candidate for the current considered point is determined by the weighted average of the depths in neighborhood. We build a criterion to find an optimal solution for the current pixel. After all pixels are processed, a higher-resolution depth map is obtained.

Applications and result analysis for the approach are also presented in this paper. With different levels of down sampled depth points, higher-resolution depth maps are generated with a generally used regular grid interpolation approach and the image guided depth super-resolution algorithm presented in this paper. After comparing and analyzing, we can see that our algorithm can generate a higher-resolution depth map with more accurate and clearer objects and boundaries than generally used interpolation approaches..

Index Terms—XR, Super-Resolution, Depth Map, Spacewarp, Timewarp, 3D Re-projection, Image Guided Super-Resolution, Head Mounted Device(HMD)

# I. INTRODUCTION

Currently, in the market, there are typically two types of XR devices. Those that are wire tethered to a PC devices and those that are self-contained, (stand-alone). Recently with the introduction of 5G, WiGig (IEEE 802.11ay), and other high speed low latency wireless protocols, there has been a movement to support a wireless solution where rendering power is remote from the XR headset/glasses. Despite the promised potential for near real-time communication with 60gig hertz wireless protocols, latency will still be an issue as image size and resolution continue to increase. As such, a form of depth based image re-projection (a.k.a: Spacearp) [7], [8], [11], [19] will be needed to correct for image inaccuracy due to lag. Depth based re-projection is dependent on both good color as well as depth information to create a new image for the display. The severity of artifacts based on occlusion is amplified as lag between the remote rendered image and arrival on the HMD. A strategy to reduce lag is to compress the color and depth image for wireless transport to the HMD.

As such, an approach to compress depth information without too much loss of accuracy is an approach called 'binning'. In this approach, instead of sending a full resolution depth map, only every 2, 4, 8, etc sub-sampled points are sent. However, this loss of information will result in other artifacts if simple averaging is used to reconstruct depth information and apply during re-projection.

In this paper we propose a unique and novel approach using available depth information as well as information from the color image to assist in a superior reconstruction of the subsampled depth map.

There are various kinds of approaches for depth superresolution. Deep learning based algorithms [1], [2], [6], [9], [12], [13], [15], [17], [18] are showing promise, however, false positive depths may lead a variety of artifacts in 3D reconstruction and 3D re-projection of XR applications. Various depth cleaning strategies and approaches are needed to remove the false depths. There are also approaches for depth super resolution for depths from RGB-D depth sensor [10], [14], [16], [18]. The algorithms also need to deal with noise from depth sensor limitations such as a low spatial resolution and ambient light saturation. In XR applications, we do not need to generate sparse depths. They are obtained by downsampling a full depth map in previous steps. The full depth map and AR objects are generated by the XR pipeline. We focus on a more accurate and less compute intensive approach to recovering a higher-resolution map from down sampled lower-resolution depth and the corresponding color image and the algorithm can be conveniently implemented on GPU to off-load CPU on mobile devices. The problem is related to depth processing including interpolation, enhancement, densification, denoising [5] [3], [4].

We present an image guided depth super-resolution algorithm to recover a higher-resolution and higher-quality depth map with a lower-resolution map and corresponding higher-resolution color image. In this algorithm, three weights are created with spatial and intensity information from the higher-resolution color image and depth information estimated from the lower-resolution depth map. The weights are created employing a Gaussian kernel such that the algorithm can remove noise during super-resolution. Also since the filtering is performed in both spatial and intensity domains, it can preserve edges and contours of the super-resolution depth map which is one of the main advantages compared with generally

used interpolation approaches. The depth estimation of the considered point is the weighted average of the depths in neighboring points. An optimization process is also created to obtain an optimal solution for the considered point.

We have organized the paper in the following manner: In Section II, we introduce the work flow of our approach. The details of image guided depth super-resolution are described in Section III. Applications and result analysis are discussed in Section IV. A summary of the paper is given in Section V.

# II. WORK FLOW OF THE APPROACH

Fig. 1 shows a work flow of the image guided depth super-resolution process for recovering a higher-resolution depth map from lower-resolution map. On device side, a higher-resolution color image and down-sampled depth map are transmitted to HMD through WiFi. Down-sampling the depth map is beneficial for reducing load in data transmission. Once the color and depth data is received on the HMD, we build a camera model with estimated focal lens and principal point with frame sizes in lower-resolution and higher-resolution, and then, compute 3D poses with the camera model and depth maps in lower-resolution and higher-resolution, in which the higher-resolution depth map is estimated at this stage by up-scaling with traditional interpolation approaches such as nearest neighbor or bi-linear interpolation from the lower-resolution, after that, create a color texture map with the color image, and finally, compute depth of each point in the higher-resolution map with 3D poses and the color texture.

In the process of computing the depth of each point in the higher-resolution map, we first compute and normalize the differences of 3D poses, color texture, and spatial distances of neighboring points, and then compute depth weight with normalized differences of 3D poses, color weight with normalized differences of color texture, and spatial weight with normalized spatial distance differences. We construct these weights with Gaussian distribution of nearby points. $\sigma_{d}$ used by depth weight is computed with the higher-resolution 3D pose. We accumulate the weights and weighted depths of neighboring points. The candidate of depth for the current considered point can be obtained with a weighted average of the depths of neighboring points.

For the depth candidate, if it is a good enough, we use it as a solution of the current considered point. Otherwise, we update 3D poses with the computed depth and repeat the process to find a better solution.

Finally, after we obtain the higher-resolution depth map, we can perform 3D re-projection of Spacewarp to get a reprojected image. We will describe each section of the approach in detail in next sessions.

# B. Image Guided Depth Super-Resolution

Similar to bilateral filtering [4], [5] in image processing by filtering image in spatial and intensity domains while smoothing image and preserving edges, we reconstruct depth of pixel $p$ in higher-resolution with spatial, intensity, and depth information of its neighborhood. The spatial and intensity information can be provided by corresponding color image and the depth information can be provided by estimated 3D poses from lower-resolution depths and an assumed camera model. The output depth is a weighted average of the input in its neighborhood and the weights can be computed from spatial, intensity, and depth information,

$$
T F (D _ {p}) = \frac {1}{W _ {p}} \sum_ {q \in N} W _ {s} W _ {r} W _ {d} D _ {q}, \tag {1}
$$

where,

$D_{p}$ is depth at pixel $p$

$D_{q}$ is depth at pixel $q\in N$ $N$ is neighborhood of pixel $p$ $V_{p}$ is accumulation of weighted depths,

$$
V _ {p} = \sum_ {q \in N} W _ {s} W _ {r} W _ {d} D _ {q}, \tag {2}
$$

$W_{p}$ is accumulation of weights,

$$
W _ {p} = \sum_ {q \in N} W _ {s} W _ {r} W _ {d}, \tag {3}
$$

$W_{s}$ is the weight constructed from spatial information of color image,

$$
W _ {s} = G _ {\sigma_ {s}} (| | p - q | |), \tag {4}
$$

$p, q$ are coordinates of pixels $p$ and $q$ respectively,

$W_{r}$ is the weight constructed from intensity information of color image,

$$
W _ {r} = G _ {\sigma_ {r}} \left(I _ {p} - I _ {q}\right), \tag {5}
$$

$I_{p}, I_{q}$ are intensities of the color image at pixels $p$ and $q$ respectively,

$W_{d}$ is the weight constructed from depth information of the depth map,

$$
W _ {d} = G _ {\sigma_ {d}} \left(D _ {p} - D _ {q}\right), \tag {6}
$$

$D_{p}, D_{q}$ are depths at pixels $p$ and $q$ respectively, and $G_{\sigma}(x)$ is a Gaussian smoothing kernel,

$$
G _ {\sigma} (x) = \frac {1}{2 \pi \sigma^ {2}} \exp \left(- \frac {x ^ {2}}{2 \sigma^ {2}}\right). \tag {7}
$$

$\sigma$ is a parameter defining the extension of the neighborhood which refers image edge blurring.

So the output depth for pixel $p$ can be obtained by,

$$
D _ {p} = \frac {V _ {p}}{W _ {p}}. \tag {8}
$$

In implementation, $W_{d}$ is computed with 3D poses estimated from depth map and an assumed camera model.

# V. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS

We have presented an image guided depth super-resolution algorithm for Spacearp which takes an important role to maintain target frame rates in XR applications. Different from traditional approaches such as bi-linear and nearest neighbor interpolation which only uses spatial information from neighboring points, the image guided depth super-resolution approach uses spatial, intensity, and depth information to construct depths of the considered points. Three weights are computed with the three kinds of information. The depth of the considered point is a weighted average of depths in neighborhood. The depth map is applied to generate new frames with new head poses based on reference frames. In order to make a comparison, we also generate new frames with depth maps created with bi-linear interpolation. From the results, we can see that depth maps generated by the image guided depth super-resolution algorithm and color images generated by Spacearp using the depth maps are well reconstructed. Comparing with the results generated with bilinear interpolation, the depth maps and color images are clearer and edges and contours of objects are well preserved. The color images have better visual quality.
